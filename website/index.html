01 — Story

The long version

I started in horror films. Or more precisely, in the question of why horror films matter — why the giallo cycle of 1970s Italian cinema, all knife-gloves and rain-slick cobblestones, was doing real intellectual work on late-capitalist urban space. That's what my dissertation at Brown was about. I spent most of my twenties writing it and teaching alongside it, and then sixteen years in higher education across film studies, digital media, critical theory, and film production — Brown, peer institutions, with a stretch as senior policy and communications strategist to the president of Rhode Island School of Design in the middle of it.

The connecting tissue, looking back, was structure. Not film as decoration, but film as a system of conventions, conventions as a system of pressures, pressures as a system of economic and political conditions. Lefebvre on the production of space, Harvey on accumulation, Soja's third space, Jameson on cultural logic. You can read horror through that lens and the films tell you things about cities and labor and fear that they don't quite know they're telling. The instinct — to look at what people present as inevitable and ask what the structure underneath actually is — turned out to travel.

At RISD I spent two years doing communications and policy work for the president across art, design, technology, and DEI portfolios. I founded the institution's first Social Equity Action Committee and Plan. I'd never thought of myself as a governance person, but RISD taught me that institutional design — who decides what, who owns accountability for what, what gets durable structure versus ad-hoc improvisation — is architectural thinking in a different medium.

In 2017 I made the engineering pivot. General Assembly's software-engineering immersive, then six years at Thought Industries — an enterprise learning-management SaaS company. I came in as an application engineer writing Node and Ember and GraphQL alongside the product team. Within two years I'd built the post-sale technical-services function from inside engineering, because no such function existed and customer-facing technical work was falling to me by default. I formalized it into Solutions Architecture, took over Sales Engineering in 2021, and was named director of the combined organization. By the end I was running 50–60 customer implementations a year across a multi-level team of solutions architects and platform engineers.

What I learned at Thought Industries is the diagnosis that became my work at Workato: a high-technical SaaS product can't be customer-supported by a Customer Success team patched into engineering through tickets. It needs a customer-facing technical organization that can absorb integration work, anchor adoption, and escalate cleanly. The structural shape of that organization is the difference between a platform that scales and one that doesn't.

Workato hired me as a Strategic Advisor in 2023. The role is two halves bolted together. The institutional half: I'm the primary architect of GEARS — Workato's customer governance and maturity framework, and of its embedded-integration variant — the methodology the company uses to take enterprises from initial implementation through governed adoption. GEARS is the institutional vocabulary; it's how the company thinks about customer scale. The hands-on half: I lead the strategic engagements at our largest enterprise customers, designing the governance, the lifecycle architecture, and increasingly the agentic-AI patterns they need to grow their automation practices across their organizations. I'm a core contributor to Workato's October 2025 enterprise Model Context Protocol launch.

It's not a coincidence that I write frameworks and read film theory. Both ask the same question — what structure is under this — from different ends of the telescope. The Argento dissertation and GEARS are closer cousins than they look.

I'm based in Barcelona now, with my family and our dog Sahi. The move is mid-flight; we're targeting full residency by the end of 2026. If you want to talk — about platforms, governance, agentic systems, 1970s Italian horror cinema, or anything in the cracks — I'm reachable through the Contact tab.

Enterprise governance and architectural judgment

Define and defend the principles that determine whether an enterprise automation program is safe at scale: identity and access, environment lifecycle, separation of duties, integration patterns, audit posture. The decisions that matter rarely reduce to a technology choice; they come down to whether the structure underneath holds under regulatory scrutiny, organizational growth, and ten years of staff turnover. On a Fortune 500 enablement program I redirected the customer's lift-and-shift instinct toward event-driven messaging on long-running workflows, locking in scale before it became a production problem. On a separate migration I caught a structural mismatch between the customer's existing access-control model and the new platform target before it shipped, averting a silent environment-permission failure.
